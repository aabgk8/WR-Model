  ---
  Key Problems to Address

  1. Target variable is very skewed. Most college WRs get 0 NFL fantasy points (undrafted, practice squad, cut). This makes regression hard — the model will
  learn "predict ~0 for everyone" and still get decent R².

  2. Random 80/20 split leaks information. Players from the same draft class share era-level signal (rule changes, scheme trends). You should validate by
  leaving out entire draft classes, not random rows.

  3. Draft capital (ROUND, SELECTION) is leakage if your goal is pre-draft prediction. If you want to identify sleepers before the draft, you can't use where
   they were drafted as a feature. If you're trying to predict post-draft, it's fine.

  4. Random Forest is a reasonable start, but gradient boosting (XGBoost/LightGBM) consistently wins on tabular data with this kind of feature set.

  ---
  Recommended Approach

  Here's how I'd structure the project:

  Step 1: Clean up the target variable

  Instead of raw career_fpts_per_game across an entire career, consider:
  - Best single-season fpts/game in first 3 NFL years — less noise from injuries/team changes later in career
  - Binary classification: "Did this player ever finish as a top-36 fantasy WR in a season?" (hit/bust)
  - Or keep regression but filter out players with < 1 NFL game so you're only predicting among players who actually got a chance

  Step 2: Leave-one-class-out cross-validation

  Train on draft classes 2015-2022, predict 2023. Then train on 2015-2023, predict 2024. This gives you honest out-of-sample error.

  Step 3: Gradient Boosted Trees (XGBoost or LightGBM)

  These handle missing values natively, are less sensitive to feature scaling, and typically outperform RF on structured data. Use early stopping on the
  validation fold to avoid overfitting.

  Step 4: Feature engineering improvements

  - Per-game rates (yards per route run is already there — great feature)
  - Breakout age / dominance share — what % of team targets did they command?
  - Year-over-year improvement (trend from freshman to final year)
  - Conference tier — SEC/Big Ten WR stats mean more than FCS stats

  Step 5: Proper evaluation

  - Ranking correlation (Spearman) — do the predicted rankings match actual rankings?
  - Precision at top-K — of the top 10 predicted WRs, how many actually hit?
  - MAE alongside R² — how many fpts/game off are you on average?
